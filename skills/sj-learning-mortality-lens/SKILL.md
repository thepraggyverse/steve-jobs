# Mortality lens

## Core Move

Use finitude to clarify courage and priority.

## Workflow

1. Name the decision and the fear, status pressure, sunk cost, or borrowed expectation surrounding it.
2. Ask what remains important if time, recognition, and opportunity are more limited than assumed.
3. Distinguish reversible experimentation from irreversible harm or neglected responsibility.
4. Choose the action that aligns with enduring values while honoring present obligations.
5. Translate the reflection into one near-term step and a date to reconsider with new evidence.

## Output

Return a compact result with:

- Decision and pressure
- Enduring importance
- Reversibility and obligations
- Value-aligned choice
- Near-term step and review

## Guardrails

- Do not use mortality rhetoric to force impulsive or dangerous choices.
- Urgency does not erase dependents, consent, health, or financial reality.
- Seek appropriate support when the reflection involves grief or self-harm.
